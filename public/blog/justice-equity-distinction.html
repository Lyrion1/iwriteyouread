Justice vs. Equity: A Necessary Distinction

December 20, 2024 • By Alexander Afolabi

The conflation of justice and equity has muddied our political discourse. This essay draws clear lines between these concepts and argues for precision in how we talk about fairness in society.

In contemporary political discourse, "justice" and "equity" are often used interchangeably. This is a mistake. While related, these concepts point to different—sometimes competing—visions of fairness. Understanding the distinction is crucial for clear thinking about policy.

Defining Justice

Justice, in its classical sense, means treating like cases alike. It emphasizes procedural fairness, equal application of rules, and merit-based outcomes. A just system is one where the same standards apply to everyone, regardless of background or identity. Justice is blind—that's the virtue of justice.

Understanding Equity

Equity, by contrast, focuses on outcomes. It recognizes that treating everyone identically may perpetuate inequality if people start from different positions. Equity seeks to level the playing field by providing different support to different groups. Where justice is blind, equity looks carefully at circumstances and adjusts accordingly.

The Tension

The tension between justice and equity is real. Consider college admissions. Justice suggests using the same criteria for all applicants—test scores, grades, achievements. Equity suggests considering historical disadvantages and providing preference to those who've overcome obstacles. Both perspectives have merit, but they can lead to incompatible policies.

Or consider hiring. Justice demands selection based solely on qualifications. Equity might require considering diversity to address historical underrepresentation. Again, reasonable people can disagree about which principle should take precedence.

The Danger of Conflation

When we conflate justice and equity, we obscure these trade-offs. We pretend there's a single, obvious answer when there isn't. Worse, we vilify those who prioritize one value over the other, treating principled disagreement as moral failure.

Someone who emphasizes justice isn't necessarily indifferent to inequality. Someone who emphasizes equity isn't necessarily abandoning fairness. They're making different judgments about how to balance legitimate competing values.

Moving Forward

What we need is honesty about the trade-offs. Justice and equity both matter. Sometimes they align—procedural fairness can promote better outcomes. Sometimes they conflict—in which case we must make difficult choices about which to prioritize.

These choices should be made transparently, with clear reasoning, acknowledging costs and benefits. What we shouldn't do is pretend the tension doesn't exist, or that preferring one value makes you virtuous while preferring the other makes you villainous.

Justice and equity are both worthy goals. But they're different goals, and clarity requires we distinguish them. Only then can we have honest conversations about how to build a fair society.
